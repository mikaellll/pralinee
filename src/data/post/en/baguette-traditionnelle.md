A global symbol of France, the **Traditional Baguette** (Baguette de Tradition) has been protected by a decree since 1993. Unlike the standard baguette, it contains no additives and relies on a long fermentation process that gives it its unique aromas, crunchy crust, and honeycomb crumb.

### The History of the 1993 Decree

Faced with the massive industrialization of bakeries in the 70s and 80s, the quality of French bread was threatened by the excessive use of additives and chemical yeasts. To preserve pure craftsmanship, the "Bread Decree" was established on September 13, 1993. This text stipulates that the "French traditional baguette" can only be made with four ingredients: wheat flour, water, salt, and yeast (or sourdough). No cold treatment is allowed beyond a certain stage, forcing the baker to a meticulous daily task that respects the natural cycles of the dough.

---

## 🛠️ Technical Sheet

- **Preparation time:** 45 minutes
- **Resting time (Bulk fermentation + Proofing):** 4.5 to 5 hours
- **Cooking time:** 20 minutes
- **Difficulty:** ⭐⭐ (Medium)
- **Servings:** 3 baguettes

## 🛒 Ingredients

- **500g** T65 wheat flour (Tradition flour)
- **350ml** pure water (Base temperature 60°C: air T° + flour T° + water T°)
- **9g** fine salt (preferably sea salt)
- **5g** fresh baker's yeast (or 3g dry yeast)
- **Optional:** 50g liquid sourdough for more flavor

---

## 👨‍🍳 Detailed Method (Step by Step)

### Step 1: Autolyse (The Secret of the Bubbles)

Mix the flour and water in a bowl until you get a homogeneous dough without lumps. Cover with a damp cloth and let rest for **30 to 45 minutes**. This step allows the gluten to structure itself naturally, facilitating kneading and improving the crumb structure.

### Step 2: Kneading

Add the crumbled yeast and salt. Knead by hand for about 10-12 minutes or in a mixer (slow speed) for 8 minutes. The dough should become smooth, elastic, and pull away from the sides. It should reach an ideal final temperature of **24°C**.

### Step 3: Bulk Fermentation (First Rise)

Place the dough in a lightly oiled container. Let rise for **2.5 to 3 hours** at room temperature (22-24°C). **Essential:** Perform a "fold" (folding the dough over itself) after 45 minutes and 1.5 hours to give strength and body to your future bread.

### Step 4: Division and Resting

Divide the dough into 3 pieces of about 250g. Shape them into light balls without degassing them too much. Let rest for 20 minutes on a floured work surface (resting), covered with a cloth.

### Step 5: Shaping

Lightly flatten a piece of dough and fold it in half lengthwise. Seal the edge with the palm of your hand. Repeat the operation to get a cylinder. Then roll gently with both hands, starting from the center and moving toward the ends, to give the desired length (about 45-50cm).

### Step 6: Proofing (Second Rise)

Place the baguettes on a floured "couche" (linen cloth), forming folds to separate them. Let rise for **1 to 1.5 hours**. The dough should double in volume and slowly spring back when pressed with a finger.

### Step 7: Baking and Scoring (Grignage)

Preheat your oven to **250°C** (th. 8-9) with an empty tray at the bottom. When putting the bread in, pour a glass of water onto the hot tray to create steam (steam shot). Score the baguettes with a razor blade, making 5 clean and regular oblique incisions.

## 💡 Chef's Tips

- **Water:** Do not use chlorinated water; prefer filtered or spring water so as not to inhibit the yeast.
- **Steam:** It is the steam that allows the crust to be thin, shiny, and crispy. Without it, the bread will be dull and the crust thick.
- **Baking:** If you prefer a "well-baked" baguette, push it to 22-24 minutes.
